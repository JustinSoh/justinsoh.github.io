ELECTRONIC DOOR DISPLAY

Project for The University of Applied Sciences . Karlsruhe, Germany



During our six-month exchange program at the University of Applied Sciences in Karlsruhe, Germany, in our third year of Polytechnic, my partner and I were assigned the Electronic Door Display project.

The objective was to develop a working prototype that would eliminate the manual process of updating classroom schedules.

Our solution involved creating a system using an ESP32 microcontroller programmed in C++. This system would automatically retrieve the daily class schedule for a specific room while concurrently implementing a theft detection mechanism.

To achieve this, we leveraged the unique dual-core feature of the ESP32, allowing us to create two parallel tasks: schedule retrieval and theft detection.

For schedule retrieval, we utilized a RESTful API to fetch the class schedule from the server and employed the Mini Grafx library to display the information on a WaveShare E-Paper Display.



In addition to schedule retrieval, we implemented a simple theft detection mechanism using a piezo buzzer and a button. The button, integrated into the enclosure and flush with the wall, triggers the buzzer when pressed. Releasing the button, indicating removal from the wall, triggers the buzzer to sound.

To ensure seamless integration with the classroom environment, we designed and 3D printed a custom enclosure that fit snugly and aesthetically on the wall. Throughout the development process, we prioritized efficiency, optimizing power consumption to extend battery life to nearly 30 days before requiring recharging.

One aspect for potential improvement is the implementation of a dashboard to monitor battery life. A system could have been implemented to estimate power consumption more accurately, enabling proactive battery replacement without disruption to classroom activities.
